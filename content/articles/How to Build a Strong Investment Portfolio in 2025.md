            <p>In 2026, the financial landscape continues to shift rapidly — with changing interest rates, evolving tax norms, and new investment products entering the market. Building a strong investment portfolio isn't about chasing the hottest stock or the latest crypto trend. It's about designing a resilient, diversified, and goal‑aligned structure that weathers volatility and delivers consistent long‑term wealth.</p>
            <p>Whether you are a first‑time investor or have been managing your own money for years, this guide walks you through the core principles of portfolio construction — asset allocation, risk management, instrument selection, rebalancing, and behavioural discipline — so you can stay ahead in 2026.</p>

            <!-- Types of asset classes (styled like home loan types) -->
            <h2>📌 Core Asset Classes for 2026</h2>
            <p>Every strong portfolio starts with the right building blocks. Below are the primary asset classes you should consider, along with their role and trade‑offs.</p>

            <div class="loan-type">
                <strong>🌐 Equities (Growth Engine)</strong><br>
                Shares of companies listed on stock exchanges. Best for long‑term wealth creation (7+ years).<br>
                <div class="pro-con">
                    <span>✔ High return potential (12-14% CAGR historically)</span>
                    <span>✔ Beats inflation consistently</span>
                    <span>✘ High volatility in short term</span>
                    <span>✘ Needs patience & discipline</span>
                </div>
            </div>

            <div class="loan-type">
                <strong>🏦 Fixed Income (Stability & Income)</strong><br>
                Includes PPF, EPF, corporate bonds, debt mutual funds, and tax‑free bonds. Provides predictable returns and reduces overall portfolio risk.<br>
                <div class="pro-con">
                    <span>✔ Capital protection (in sovereign instruments)</span>
                    <span>✔ Regular interest income</span>
                    <span>✘ Lower returns than equity over long term</span>
                    <span>✘ Interest rate risk (bond prices fall when rates rise)</span>
                </div>
            </div>

            <div class="loan-type">
                <strong>🥇 Gold (Hedge Against Uncertainty)</strong><br>
                Sovereign Gold Bonds (SGBs) or Gold ETFs. Acts as a hedge against inflation and geopolitical risks.<br>
                <div class="pro-con">
                    <span>✔ Negative correlation with equities (often)</span>
                    <span>✔ No wealth tax, easy to hold</span>
                    <span>✘ No regular income (no interest/dividend except SGB interest)</span>
                    <span>✘ Price can stay flat for years</span>
                </div>
            </div>

            <div class="loan-type">
                <strong>🏗️ Real Assets (REITs & InvITs)</strong><br>
                Real Estate Investment Trusts and Infrastructure Investment Trusts provide exposure to commercial real estate or infrastructure projects with relatively lower ticket size.<br>
                <div class="pro-con">
                    <span>✔ Attractive dividend yields (5–8%)</span>
                    <span>✔ Listed on exchanges — liquid</span>
                    <span>✘ Market volatility like equities</span>
                    <span>✘ Tax treatment less favourable than equity</span>
                </div>
            </div>

            <div class="highlight">
                💡 <strong>Which asset allocation suits you?</strong> For investors under 40 with stable income, a 60-70% equity, 20-25% debt, and 5-10% gold/REITs is a classic growth portfolio. Near retirement, shift to 40% equity, 50% debt, 10% cash/gold. Always align with your risk appetite.
            </div>

            <h2>⚖️ Risk Management — Parameters That Protect Your Portfolio</h2>
            <p>Your risk management strategy determines whether a market crash destroys your wealth or becomes a buying opportunity. Below are the key parameters every investor should track.</p>
            <table>
                <thead><tr><th>Parameter</th><th>Ideal Practice</th><th>Impact on Portfolio</th></tr></thead>
                <tbody>
                    <tr><td>Asset Allocation Drift</td><td>Rebalance when any asset deviates >5–10% from target</td><td>Prevents overexposure to overheated sectors</td></tr>
                    <tr><td>Emergency Fund</td><td>6 months of living expenses in liquid funds / savings</td><td>Prevents forced selling during downturns</td></tr>
                    <tr><td>Concentration Risk</td><td>No single stock >5% of equity portfolio; max 20% in one sector</td><td>Reduces blow‑up risk from company‑specific failures</td></tr>
                    <tr><td>Volatility Tolerance</td><td>Portfolio should not lose more than 15-20% in a severe bear market</td><td>Ensures you stay invested without panic</td></tr>
                </tbody>
            </table>

            <h2>📋 Step-by-Step: How to Build Your Portfolio in 2026</h2>
            <div class="steps">
                <div class="step"><div class="step-number">1</div><div><strong>Set clear financial goals & horizon</strong><br>Retirement (15+ years), child education (10 years), or house down payment (5 years). Each goal needs a separate asset mix.</div></div>
                <div class="step"><div class="step-number">2</div><div><strong>Assess your risk profile</strong><br>Use a risk assessment questionnaire. Be honest: would you panic if your portfolio drops 20%? Adjust equity exposure accordingly.</div></div>
                <div class="step"><div class="step-number">3</div><div><strong>Choose a strategic asset allocation</strong><br>Example: 60% equity (Nifty 50 + mid cap), 25% debt (PPF + dynamic bond fund), 10% gold (SGB), 5% REITs.</div></div>
                <div class="step"><div class="step-number">4</div><div><strong>Select low‑cost, tax‑efficient instruments</strong><br>Use index ETFs/ direct mutual funds for equity, sovereign gold bonds for gold, and AAA-rated bonds/Bharat Bond ETFs for debt.</div></div>
                <div class="step"><div class="step-number">5</div><div><strong>Implement systematically (SIP / lump sum)</strong><br>For equity, use SIPs to average out volatility. For debt, lump sum works well when interest rates are peaking.</div></div>
                <div class="step"><div class="step-number">6</div><div><strong>Monitor and rebalance every 6–12 months</strong><br>If equity has grown to 70% of portfolio, sell some and move to debt/gold to lock in gains.</div></div>
                <div class="step"><div class="step-number">7</div><div><strong>Keep investment costs and taxes low</strong><br>Prefer direct plans over regular (lower expense ratio). Use indexation benefit on debt funds if holding >3 years.</div></div>
            </div>

            <h2>❓ Frequently Asked Questions</h2>
            <div class="faq-item"><div class="faq-question">What is the ideal number of mutual funds in a portfolio?</div><div>For most retail investors, 4–6 funds are sufficient: one large cap index fund, one mid cap fund, one flexicap, one aggressive hybrid or debt fund, and one gold ETF. More funds lead to overlap and complexity without added diversification.</div></div>
            <div class="faq-item"><div class="faq-question">How often should I rebalance my portfolio?</div><div>Rebalance every 6–12 months or whenever an asset class moves more than 5–10% away from its target allocation. Avoid frequent tinkering – it increases transaction costs and taxes. Use inflows (new SIPs) to rebalance gradually.</div></div>
            <div class="faq-item"><div class="faq-question">Are index ETFs better than active mutual funds in 2026?</div><div>For most investors, low‑cost index ETFs or index direct plans have been outperforming a majority of active funds over 10+ years. Unless you have a strong view on a specific manager, index funds offer simplicity and lower expense ratios (0.1–0.3% vs 0.8–1.2% for active).</div></div>
            <div class="faq-item"><div class="faq-question">How much gold should I hold in my portfolio?</div><div>Typically 5–10% of total portfolio as a hedge against equity downturns and currency depreciation. Sovereign Gold Bonds (SGBs) are the most tax‑efficient way. Avoid physical gold unless for personal use (high making charges and storage risk).</div></div>
            <div class="faq-item"><div class="faq-question">What is the biggest mistake investors make in 2026?</div><div>Chasing past performance – buying the fund that gave 40% last year, then selling after a 10% dip. Another common mistake: keeping too much idle cash (savings account) or having no emergency fund, forcing panic selling during market corrections.</div></div>
